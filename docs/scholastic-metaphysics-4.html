Time to see how accurate they are as I read the text, which as always, I will rely to as I read:

  1. What is the point of stopping to discuss the special characteristics of the concepts we use in metaphysics?

    Otherwise we might misuse them is the gist as I understood it.

    • What does it mean that a concept is a “transcendental” one?

      It is all-inclusive to the things it describes.1

    • Why is being such?

      Because if something isn’t then it doesn’t exist.

    • Why is it said to be at once the “poorest” and the “richest” of all concepts?

      Looks like I was right. It’s the poorest because it doesn’t actually specify anything, but the richest because it describes everything.

  3. Most ordinary concepts we use are formed by abstracting a common essence and omitting particular details.

    • Why is it that being cannot be thus formed?

      To omit a detail is to remove it from being, which you cannot, since being encompasses everything. That is, while you can abstract sex and age from human beings, because there is a shared humanity without them, there is no way to abstract things away to get to being.

    • How then is it formed?

      “Judgement of Separation,” so basically just saying the word is. It feels a whole lot like plain abstraction to me, though.

    • How do the three types of concepts differ from each other: univocal, equivocal, analogous?

      Univocal concepts can be rigidly applied to multiple beings with the same meaning. Ex: all hydrogen atoms can be described as such.

      Equivocal concepts are homonyms, which is annoying. Ex: he put his money in the bank which was on the riverbank.

      Analogous concepts are between the two, similar in meaning but not identical. Ex: Strength of arm, muscle, and will.

    • Why is it necessary that the main concepts used in metaphysics be analogous?

      Because we use the concept of being, and because we’re generally talking about similar, not identical things.

  5. Analogy of Attribution:

    • What does this mean?

      Things get descriptions not because they are but because they are related to things which are.

    • Examples?

      Healthy food. Food is dead, so not healthy, but makes healthy people healthy, so is seen as healthy. American cars lack citizenship but are made (in theory) in and by Americans.

    • Does it express any intrinsic similarity between the prime analogate and the secondary anlogates?

      Yes, the secondary gains the term because of the prime.

    • Why do we use it?

      We shouldn’t.

  6. Analogy of Proportionality:

    • Meaning?

      Things are alike in how they do the same thing.

    • Examples?

      Man and mice both know.

  7. What is the basis in reality for all uses of analogy of proportionality?

    Action is the thing which bonds beings, and so since proportionality is based on shared action...

    • What is happening in an analogy of Metaphorical or Improper Proportionality?

      Use of metaphor.

    • Examples?

      He’s a snake.

    • Why is it not literally true?

      Men aren’t snakes.

    • What is the purpose of using it then?

      Sounds better than “Like a snake, he is sneaky.” Poetry is good.

  9. Analogy of Proper Proportionality:

    • Meaning?

      Seems like the general example from above with men and mice both knowing.

    • Examples?

      Men and mice know differently, but both still know.

    • Explain how such an analogous term can be at once literally true of all its analogates, yet shifting somewhat in meaning for each, i.e., how can it be so flexible, as an univocal cannot?

      Words have shifted meaning based on context.

  10. Does an analogous concept contain two parts, one of which expresses just the similarity, the other just the diversity?

    No, that would be a univocal and equivocal concept paired together. Instead, it encompasses both at once, expressing the shared and not.

  11. Analogy Applied to Being: How does this work, since being seems to be such a simple concept and not expressing any common action, as other proper proportionality concepts do?

    The common action is existence.

  12. Analogy Applied to God: How does it avoid falling into anthropomorphism (making God too much like humans) on the one hand, or mere metaphor on the other?

    It says that since the common action is existence, rather than existence being separate from essence, since the Almighty exists, He has substance, and shares that with us. However, sharing existence is all that is claimed, avoiding anthropomorphism.

  13. Why is William of Ockham forced to hold that the concept of being applies univocally to both God and creatures, whereas St. Thomas is not?

    He focuses on being as essence, rather including existence.

  14. How does St. Thomas ground the real similarity between God and creatures, making possible a proper analogy between them?

    He says that they share existence.
